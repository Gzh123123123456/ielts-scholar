# IELTS Writing Task 2 Frameworks

## Purpose
Define standard paragraph plans and common failures for each Task 2 question type. Used in Phase 1 (Framework Discussion) and Phase 3 (Feedback) to evaluate essay structure.

## Framework Types

### 1. Opinion ("To what extent do you agree or disagree?")
**Required response:** A clear position (agree, disagree, or balanced) stated in the introduction and maintained throughout. Body paragraphs support the position. Conclusion restates position.

**Paragraph plan:**
- Intro: Paraphrase question + state position
- Body 1: First reason + example
- Body 2: Second reason + example (or counter-argument + rebuttal for balanced approach)
- Conclusion: Restate position + summary

**Common failure:** The candidate sits on the fence without a clear stance, writing "I partly agree" then never clarifying what they disagree with.

### 2. Discuss Both Views
**Required response:** Summarize both viewpoints fairly, then give your own opinion. Both views must be developed with roughly equal depth.

**Paragraph plan:**
- Intro: Paraphrase + state that both views will be discussed
- Body 1: View A + reasoning + example
- Body 2: View B + reasoning + example
- Body 3 (or merged into Body 2): Your own opinion with reasoning
- Conclusion: Summarize + restate your opinion

**Common failure:** One view gets a full paragraph while the other gets a single sentence. The candidate's own opinion is unclear or missing.

### 3. Advantages / Disadvantages
**Required response:** Discuss both advantages and disadvantages. May ask "Do the advantages outweigh the disadvantages?" — must answer this explicitly.

**Paragraph plan:**
- Intro: Paraphrase + indicate whether advantages outweigh
- Body 1: Advantages (2–3 points, developed)
- Body 2: Disadvantages (2–3 points, developed)
- Conclusion: Weigh both sides + final judgment

**Common failure:** Listing advantages and disadvantages without weighing them against each other.

### 4. Problem / Solution
**Required response:** Identify problems caused by a situation and propose solutions. Solutions must be specific, not vague ("the government should do something").

**Paragraph plan:**
- Intro: Paraphrase + outline that problems and solutions will be discussed
- Body 1: Problem 1 + explanation; Problem 2 + explanation
- Body 2: Solution 1 + how it works; Solution 2 + how it works
- Conclusion: Summarize + feasibility note

**Common failure:** Solutions are generic ("raise awareness", "make laws") without explaining HOW they would work.

### 5. Cause / Effect
**Required response:** Explain why something happens (causes) and what results from it (effects). Do not confuse this with Problem/Solution.

**Paragraph plan:**
- Intro: Paraphrase + state that causes and effects will be examined
- Body 1: Cause 1 + explanation; Cause 2 + explanation
- Body 2: Effect 1 + explanation; Effect 2 + explanation
- Conclusion: Summarize the causal chain

**Common failure:** The candidate drifts into giving solutions instead of analyzing effects.

### 6. Two-Part Question
**Required response:** Answer both parts of the question. Both parts must be given roughly equal attention.

**Paragraph plan:**
- Intro: Paraphrase + outline how both parts will be addressed
- Body 1: Answer to Part A
- Body 2: Answer to Part B
- Conclusion: Summarize both answers

**Common failure:** One part is answered thoroughly while the other is answered in one sentence or ignored entirely.

## Framework Flexibility

The question type is a diagnostic tool, not a template command. If the prompt combines multiple demands (e.g., discuss both views AND give your opinion AND suggest solutions), prioritize answering all parts over forcing a textbook structure. A candidate who addresses every dimension of a hybrid question with a logically sound structure is doing the right thing — even if the paragraph count or order does not match a standard template.

## Feedback Rules
- In Phase 1, check whether the candidate's framework actually matches the question type.
- If the candidate chose the wrong framework, suggest the correct one and explain why.
- In Phase 3, evaluate whether the essay body matches the framework the candidate stated in Phase 1.

## Phase 1 Idea Generation Rules

Before committing to a framework, the candidate should:
- **Identify the topic** precisely. Restate it in your own words to confirm you understood all parts.
- **Define key terms** when necessary. If the question uses a vague term like "success" or "development", clarify what it means in the context of this essay.
- **Decide whether the question is binary or graded.** Is this a yes/no issue or a spectrum? What is the core conflict or tension?
- **Consider stakeholders.** Who is affected? (e.g., students, parents, employers, governments, society)
- **Consider time / place / culture dimensions** when useful. Is this issue different in urban vs rural? In developed vs developing countries? Now vs 20 years ago?
- **Build logical chains:** cause → effect → example, or problem → consequence → solution.
- **Do not leave the reader hanging.** If you mention advantages, disadvantages, benefits, or drawbacks — explain each one immediately. A list of claims without development is empty.

## Do Not
- Do not force every essay into a rigid 4-paragraph template. Some questions benefit from 5 paragraphs.
- Do not penalize a candidate for using a different but logically sound structure.
