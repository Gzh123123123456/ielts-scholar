# IELTS Speaking Part 2 Strategy

## Purpose
Guide the AI in evaluating Part 2 long-turn answers. The candidate speaks for 1–2 minutes on a given topic with cue card prompts. Answers should be structured as a spoken narrative, not a bullet list.

## Core Rules
- Use the 1-minute preparation time to write keywords only — never full sentences.
- Structure the answer as a story or extended description with a clear beginning, middle, and end.
- Cover all cue card bullet points, but weave them into natural speech rather than ticking them off.
- Use descriptive adjectives and sensory details to expand the answer.
- Aim for approximately 1 minute 30 seconds to 2 minutes.

## Feedback Rules
- Flag list-like answers: "First, it is big. Second, it is useful. Third, it is cheap." → natural narrative.
- Flag answers that run significantly under 1 minute (insufficient development).
- Flag answers that repeat the same point in different words without adding new content.
- Encourage the candidate to show, not just tell: add a specific moment or anecdote.

## Tags
`fluency`, `coherence`, `lack_of_example`, `sentence_structure`, `unclear_reference`

## Examples

### List-style (weak)
> The object is my phone. It is small and useful. I use it every day. I use it for calls and messages. I would feel lost without it.
**Issue:** No narrative flow. Sounds like reading bullet points aloud.

### Story-based (stronger)
> The one object I genuinely rely on is my phone — not just for calls, but as a sort of second brain. I remember leaving it at home once last year, and the whole day felt off, like I'd forgotten my wallet and my keys at the same time. What makes it indispensable isn't the device itself but the mental offloading it provides…

## Cue Card Usage
- Cue card bullet points are organization prompts, not a checklist to read mechanically. Do not start each sentence with "First… Second… Third…" reciting the bullets.
- Use transition language lightly and naturally — the listener should feel the story flowing, not see a scaffold.
- Do not overdecorate or invent fake dramatic details. If the cue card asks about "a useful object", a phone is fine. It does not need to be a rare artifact or a life-changing invention.

## Do Not
- Do not penalize short pauses or thinking sounds (um, uh) heavily in Part 2 — some planning during speech is natural.
- Do not expect short, concise answers. Part 2 is the one section where length is expected.
- Do not require formal paragraph structure or topic sentences.
